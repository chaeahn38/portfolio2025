Summer in Seoul is thick and heavy. But luckily, by the time I leave, I’ll be heading somewhere cooler. I’ve started getting ready for school. I’ve found a place to stay, and my embassy interview is in two weeks. My flight to Zurich is already booked. I’ve also begun studying French. Every morning, I turn on RTS, the Swiss public radio. I can barely understand the news, but I keep it on anyway. Swiss French sounds different from standard French—it’s firmer, more deliberate, like it's trying not to miss a single letter. I’m glad I know a little German; it helps. But French, especially the pronunciation, really tests your patience.

Right now, French is my weakness. Back in middle school, it was English. I remember a boy from Indonesia mocking my accent, and our teacher chuckling from behind. I think that’s when I first became overly sensitive about pronunciation whenever I learn a new language.

Even in the middle of summer, the embassy didn’t have the air conditioning on. I don’t sweat easily, so I just chugged the bottle of water I’d brought. People waiting in front of me were fanning themselves and shot me looks, as if asking me to agree with their discomfort. My turn came quickly. The woman at the counter asked about my background. I told her about my father’s job at a shoe factory under the blazing sun, and said I’m grateful to have the chance to study. When I asked if there was any way to speed up the visa process, she changed her expression and said dryly, There is no fast track. Still, the embassy building was beautiful.

An email came from Matthieu, the head of the program. It was quite long. A detailed list of tools and papers for the first class, along with a reading list. You could tell how carefully it was written.
— 01. Pilot Parallel Pen. You can get a set with multiple sizes, but it’s not required. Depending on the size of your lettering, certain pens may feel more natural in your hand. Similar tools from other brands are fine too.
— 02. A3-size loose sheets. The paper should have a smooth surface that won’t bleed ink. It doesn’t need to be expensive—just test it out beforehand.
— 03. A 30cm ruler, pencils, and an eraser.
— 04. A3 tracing paper sheets. Make sure it’s transparent enough.
— 05. Whatever pens and pencils you personally prefer.

I’ve already read several of the books—about twenty of them are coming with me. Bringhurst, Potter, Hochuli, Gill, Winger, Noordzij, Kinross. My bag is going to be heavy. I can always buy more books once I’m there, but carrying them across Zurich’s train station feels like a bad idea waiting to happen. Maybe I should scan them instead. What a mess.

Doomscrolling what to watch on Netflix — if the title doesn’t look right, I don’t even click. Netflix is today’s wall of movie posters: endlessly scrollable, instantly judged. And in that sense, it’s not so different from the lobby walls of mid-century Korean cinemas, where film flyers once hung like silent performers. Back then, title lettering didn’t just label a film — it sold it. With a few bold strokes, it conjured drama, scale, and genre. The type on those flyers wasn’t typeset — it was drawn. It was human. It was persuasion by pencil and brush.

Leaf through Korea’s foreign movie flyers from the 1950s, and that power is immediately visible. At the time, most foreign films entered Korea by way of Japan. This meant that movie titles — like Peter Pan, 1953, phonetically as Pita Pan — often passed through one layer of translation before arriving. The visual language, too, carried traces of Japanese influence: in composition, in layout, in typographic sequencing. But imitation gave way to invention. In those tight margins, Korean designers developed something distinct. Flyers juggled Korean, English, and Japanese text across a single page. The results could be chaotic, beautiful, or both — sometimes resembling blueprints for dreams. But always, they were evidence of a multilingual visual culture negotiating with itself.

This was a golden age of hand-drawn persuasion. With the postwar flood of foreign films came an explosion in flyer production. Each one was part collage, part painting: studio stills clipped and pasted, titles rendered by hand in expressive, often exaggerated forms. Typography became sculpture, metaphor, narrative. You didn’t just read these letters — you felt them.

Inside movie theaters, they had a design studio with a team of painters and letterers worked under leads called oyaji — a Japanese term loosely translating to boss or senior. Together, they built the city’s film culture one brushstroke at a time. At major theaters like Daehan and Dansungsa, towering hand-painted signs drew the crowds in. And the flyers — stacked in theater lobbies or pasted across shopfronts — spoke in a language you couldn’t scroll past.

We don’t know the names of most of the people who made them. Many were trained not in art schools but on the job — under fluorescent lights, on tight deadlines, often redrawing foreign letters they didn’t fully understand. But they made them theirs. Even when copying imported designs, something personal slipped through: a curve, a flourish, a risk.

Of course, technology changed all that. Digital printing replaced brushes, and the vector file became the new standard. But the hand-drawn era, for all its impermanence, left behind something lasting: a visual code. You could glance at a flyer and guess the genre — a jagged serif suggested a Western, a curlicued headline a period drama. Style and story were bound together. Even the paper had a certain weight of intent.

I couldn’t stop looking at these letters. So I began redrawing them. I collected favorites and created a small system of digital interpretations — about twenty characters in all. From Pita Pan, I extrapolated the rest. This was more of a revival project. In vector form, I traced the originals: how a point becomes a line, how a line becomes a curve, how a curve becomes a gesture. Of course, the results were cleaner, but I wasn’t chasing fidelity. I was studying intent. The hardest part was restraint. How do you bring structure to something built on improvisation? I borrowed typographic rules — baseline, rhythm, contrast — but kept the wildness when I could. At times, I worried I was flattening what made the originals special. Still, trying to turn those hand-drawn letters into a usable type system felt like an act of homage. I wasn’t restoring the past. I was responding to it.

What survives of mid-century Korean lettering isn’t nostalgia — it’s a record. A record of people working fast and thinking visually. A record of how language, image, and commerce collided. A record of graphic labor in its most improvisational form. Most of all, it’s a reminder that typography doesn’t always begin with the type designer.
영화 전단지에 사용된 제목 글씨는 단순한 장식이 아니다. 영화의 성격을 선명하게 드러내는 시각 요소이자, 관객의 시선을 붙잡는 첫 번째 홍보 수단이다. 지금 당신 손에 쥔 영화 전단지 속 글자체는 누군가의 손끝을 거쳐 완성된 문화 산업의 집약체다. 1950년대 외화 전단지를 들여다보면, 타이틀 레터링의 매력을 다시금 느낄 수 있다. 그 시절 영화 타이틀은 단순한 글자가 아니었다. 도시의 공기와 시대의 정서를 머금은 이미지였고, 상업적 목적의 홍보물을 넘어 하나의 문화적 산물이었다. 시간의 흐름 속에서 오히려 예술적 아름다움이 먼저 눈에 들어오게 된다. 그것은 한 시대의 타이포그래피 감각이자, 문화로 존재했던 시각적 증거다.

이 시기의 외화는 대부분 일본에서 먼저 상영된 뒤 한국으로 수입되었다. 자연스럽게 일본의 전단지를 참고해 작업하는 경우가 많았다. 디즈니의 Peter Pan이 피터 팬이 아니라 피타 판으로 표기된 것은, 일본식 발음을 그대로 차용했기 때문이다. 타이틀의 배열이나 지면 구성 방식에서도 일본 디자인의 영향이 뚜렷이 드러난다. 하지만 문화가 수입되는 그 흐름 속에서도 한국만의 독특한 시각 언어는 자생적으로 생성되었다. 이러한 작업의 결과물은 예상치 못한 방식으로 이미지와 문자, 그래픽 요소가 한 공간 안에 공존하게 만들었다. 한글, 영문, 일본어가 뒤섞인 전단지의 인쇄물 레이아웃은 때로 기상천외한 방을 닮았다. 이는 단지 디자이너의 취향이라기보다, 그 시대의 문화적 맥락과 언어 체계를 고스란히 반영한 것이다.

당시의 영화 제목 하나만 봐도, 언어와 타이포그래피가 얼마나 밀도 있게 응축되어 있는지를 알 수 있다. 1953년 한국전쟁이 끝난 직후, 외화 수입이 물밀듯 쏟아졌던 시기에 수많은 작업자들이 이 글자들을 그려 넣었다. 컬러 필름, 시네마스코프, 70mm 대형 화면 등 새로운 영상 기술이 유입되면서 국내에는 매우 다양한 영화가 개봉되었고, 그만큼 전단지 제작 역시 급증했다. 전단지는 종종 외화 사진을 오려 붙이고, 그 위에 손글씨를 그려 넣는 콜라주 형식으로 제작되었다. 이후 인쇄하여 배포했는데, 이는 당시 필동, 인현동, 충무로 등에 자리잡은 인쇄소들과 발달된 기술 덕분에 가능했던 일이었다. 이러한 작업 속에서 손글씨는 레터링 작업자의 예술적 감각을 그대로 드러낼 수 있는 가장 솔직한 매체였다. 단순히 활자를 나열하는 것이 아니라, 장식과 패턴 같은 회화적 요소를 접목해 시각적으로 돋보이게 했다. 평면에 얹히는 것이 아니라, 입체적으로 구현되기도 했다. 건축적 양식을 응용해 고풍스러운 느낌을 주거나, 돌을 깎아낸 듯한 질감으로 표현한 글자도 있었다. 글자가 영화 자체를 대변하듯, 살아 움직이는 듯한 인상을 주었다.

그러나 디지털 인쇄와 사진 기술의 발전은 손작업의 필요를 사라지게 했다. 붓과 펜촉으로 한 획씩 그려 넣던 과거의 레터링은 점차 잉크젯으로 뽑아낸 벡터 이미지로 대체되었고, 그것이 새로운 표준이 되었다. 당시 레터링은 본래 영화 홍보를 위한 상업적 목적이 분명했지만, 그 간결함과 과감한 표현력 덕분에 오히려 오늘날 더 특별하게 느껴진다. 레터링만 봐도 영화의 장르를 유추할 수 있을 정도로 강한 시각적 암시가 담겨 있었다. 날카로운 글자는 무법 시대의 서부극을, 화려한 중세풍 장식은 시대극을 떠올리게 했다. 글자의 마감, 질감, 입체감만으로도 그 방송물이 예능인지 드라마인지 로맨스물인지 감을 잡을 수 있었던 것이다.

영화관이나 수입사에는 오늘날의 디자인 스튜디오에 해당하는 안실이 따로 있었다. 포스터를 포함한 모든 영화 홍보물을 그곳에서 만들었다. 당대 대한극장, 단성사 등 유명 극장의 입구에는 웅장한 간판이 걸렸고, 그것은 영화의 흥행을 위한 직접적인 홍보 수단이었다. 관객들은 신문 광고나 극장 앞 전단지로 상영작 정보를 접했으며, 그 안의 글자들은 단지 쓴 것이 아니라, 확실히 그려진 것이었다. 간판 작업으로 이름을 남긴 이는 백춘태 화가로 알려져 있지만, 대부분의 작업물에는 크레딧이 남지 않아 작업자를 알기 어렵다. 일부 극장은 미술부를 운영하며, 유능한 간판 화가를 오야지라 불렀다. 오야지는 일본식 표현으로 형님, 반장님 정도의 의미다. 미술부장은 보조 화가와 레터링 작가들과 함께 작업했고, 보조 화가들은 도제식 체계 아래서 그 일을 배워나갔다.

수입된 전단지 샘플을 참고해 글자를 그리는 경우가 많았지만, 그 와중에도 작업자의 개성이 드러났다. 레터링은 보통 그림이 끝난 후 마지막 단계에서 공간에 맞춰 작업되었는데, 제한된 면적 안에 글자를 우겨 넣는 식으로 진행되었다. 이 과정에서 글자의 물리성이 극명하게 드러난다. 한글과 한자, 원어 제목까지 뒤섞인 긴 제목을 끼워 넣기 위해, 글자는 늘어나고 구부러지며 변주되었다. 이는 오늘날 일관성을 중시하는 디지털 서체 작업에서는 상상할 수 없는 일이지만, 당시 손글씨는 주변 환경과 함께 호흡하며 유기적으로 성장해갔다.

나는 이런 글자들에 매료되어, 인상 깊었던 글자체들을 모아 약 스무 자의 파생 글꼴을 제작했다. 피타판이라는 세 글자만 단서로 남아 있던 상태에서, 나머지 글자의 형태를 상상해 그려야 했다. 이는 과거 수작업을 디지털 인터페이스 위에서 다시 해석하는 일이었다. 디지털 디자인 프로그램 안에서 점은 선이 되고, 선은 곡선이 되며, 곡선은 윤곽을 이룬다. 화면 속 디자인은 픽셀을 통해 가장 이상적인 형태로 구현된 시뮬레이션이다. 그것은 아마 당시 간판 화가가 상상하던 모습과는 사뭇 다른 구현일 것이다. 나는 이 과정을 일종의 팬픽션처럼 여겼다. 원작에 대한 존경을 담은, 그러나 해석이 들어간 이차 창작물이었다.

A memory derived from a room where I took a nap. Lying down on the floor with my eyes closed, I recalled the space with a faint consciousness. Remembering an image of the room, I found my body naturally curled into a space between towering bookshelves. The shelves, reaching over ten feet tall, loomed three times higher than my six-year-old self. In my illiterate age, I was accustomed to gazing up at these towers, their spines engraved with letters. Surrounded by walls where books opened up like rib cages, exposing their spines, I was both beneath the wall and within it. The memory lingers with scents, confined by the bookshelf and the frigidness of the floor. The smallest details, like dust gathered on the shelves, remain vivid.

Later, I moved the bookshelf to a one-story room where I could lie in bed, gazing at it all night. I repeatedly read the authors and titles on the shelves. On fatigue days, when my eyes could barely open, I’d try to read a section, even backwards. This routine became a cycle; I read back and forth, as though rehearsing along the swimming pool rails. Spine reading is an exercise in reading with the eyes in stillness. My lips would move for a deeper dive, then pause while my eyes followed the text and titles. Reading with only the eyes created a profound silence. Even after I looked away, the silence remained.

Some read meticulously; others read obsessively. Any text that’s overly dissected loses rhythm. Yet, there’s a unique rhythm in scanning lists of train schedules or Excel tables. Titles on book spines are usually nouns or fragmented phrases. Though partial or incomplete, they are easier to imagine, drawing out a full picture from memory. As I read a title, the words form in rows. Humans, processing thought into language, approach the world linearly. Their lips move, and the world unfurls in front of them. Slowly, words hover in sound, while lists of words fold the logical mind into geometric origami. Like frost patterns on a window, images spread across the mind. Linear thinking, with its start and end, binds one thought to another.

Books, meanwhile, wait patiently in place, displaying their spines most of the time. The recollections of the mind are often dim and unorganized—unlike a bookshelf, where books stand in orderly rows. They tell of tasks completed, things past, and what we’ve lost today. Observing a book’s spine reveals titles inscribed in two ways: vertically or horizontally. Vertically written titles are read from top to bottom, while horizontal titles can be more challenging, as they’re read from left to right. In Korea, most books are printed with vertical titles, while those in Latin languages are horizontal. Horizontal titles aren’t as easy to read when viewed upright; I often had to turn my head clockwise to align my gaze with the letters. Sometimes, I’d read titles while lying in bed or even attempting a handstand. I’d shake my head left and right, shrugging my shoulders, half-joking that one day I’d publish a yoga guide to Spine Reading.

Books sit next to each other, carefully aligned. I often imagine what would happen if they weren’t. I take a closer look at the bookshelf, planning the order in which to place each book. The upper shelves align with my eye level, so I place frequently used books there. Those on lower shelves require me to bend down, offering a quiet humility in the act. Adjusting my gaze to the book’s position, I realize how crucial it is to arrange books in a way that suits their owner. Unfinished books lie open, pages paused between the gaps on the shelf. I classify books as tangible or ephemeral, single sheets or bound volumes, thick or thin, authored or anonymous, published or self-made, original or copied, light or heavy.

Small items sprout between the books—postcards, bookmarks from book fairs, concert tickets, letters, stickers, scribbled notes, gallery leaflets, and Christmas cards. There are many ways to organize a bookshelf: count the books in a row, group them by color, recall the authors, notice the publisher’s logo, consider the binding, translate titles into your language, distinguish horizontal from vertical text, skim the first and last letters, imagine an invisible slope along the books, identify fonts, and find exceptions.

I desire a stable, fixed, unmoving bookshelf. Yet such a thing doesn’t exist as long as books are opened, closed, shifted, and replaced. A bookshelf that resists its intended use is not a wall. It’s a shifting wall, a wall of memories. After dusting off the surface and aligning the books, I took a photograph to capture the moment. Books captured through a camera lens offer a glimpse of the owner's exhibitionist desire, like glancing at neighbors through a rear window. Spine reading doesn’t merely preserve the past. It isn’t simply a record; it’s an exercise that releases tension, creating a stillness filled with new meanings.

Ellen Lupton's Typographic Binaries is a relentless examination of how typography’s visual distinctions often carry assumptions about hierarchy and power. Roman versus italic, serif versus sans-serif, uppercase versus lowercase—these binary oppositions are so deeply ingrained in design that we seldom question their origins. But Lupton does. Typography, born from the invention of movable type, became the engine of mass production. Standardized typefaces rapidly transformed religion, science, literature, law, and commerce. Yet while typography propelled human progress, it also reinforced rigid structures—binary ones.

Roman type is seen as the default, while italic is relegated to a tool for emphasis or deviation. This isn’t a matter of utility alone—it reflects cultural hierarchies. If Roman is unmarked, then italic is marked. The unmarked is understood as neutral, natural, and dominant; the marked is exceptional, noticeable, even disruptive. But this wasn’t always the case. Roman and italic were originally conceived as distinct styles with different purposes. In the early 15th century, Aldus Manutius published affordable, compact books using italic type designed by Francesco Griffo. Inspired by cursive handwriting, Griffo’s italic was efficient and space-saving. Because italic capital letters were lacking, Roman capitals were often substituted.

By the 16th century, Roman had become the default for most printed texts, while italic was reserved for emphasis. Designers like Garamond developed type families that included both styles, reinforcing a binary within the structure of typography itself. Italic became visually subordinate—narrower, slanted, harmonized to match the dominant Roman form. Even the concept of a type family invites scrutiny. In reality, families are rarely uniform; they drift apart, fracture, and reconvene in messy, unpredictable ways. Typography’s version of the family, however, is more rigid—anchored in inherited forms and expectations.

Lupton reminds us that typography is never neutral. Even the contrast between white paper and black ink is loaded. The idealized white page, prized for legibility, became a metaphor for purity—one entangled with racial ideologies. In the 18th century, John Baskerville intensified the contrast between ink and paper by ironing the page’s surface and refining the finish. His contemporaries found the effect disturbing—too bright, too sharp. But what Baskerville achieved was not just technical refinement; it was a performance of authority through form. That same performance continued in the dramatic thicks and thins of Bodoni and Didot, whose typefaces made high contrast a virtue.

In 1920s Europe, modernist design—gridded architecture, sans-serif typefaces, functional photography—claimed neutrality and universality. But these gestures were, in part, reactions to nationalism and fascism. They attempted to resist dominant ideologies by appearing apolitical. Typography’s history is filled with systems and hierarchies, yet contemporary designers increasingly question these legacies. They experiment with type that refuses neat categorization. Every choice—from font to leading to paper stock—becomes loaded with cultural and political meaning.

Looking back, I wonder how many times I’ve accepted the default without question. How often have I dismissed what’s been pushed to the margins as secondary, less important? Typography creates order—but it can also dismantle it. It reproduces structures, challenges them, and invites fracture. Binary thinking may seem tidy, but it’s a veil that obscures complexity. Design language holds the power to disrupt these binaries, to redraw the map. Margins, baselines, frames, gutters—these are not just tools of layout but metaphors of power. In practice, real boundaries are leaky, shifting, and porous. They are not fixed or absolute. If we are to design responsibly, we must read these structures more critically—and learn when and how to let them collapse.

In the short story Spectrum by Korean science fiction writer Kim Choyeop, an alien civilization communicates through color. From a physics standpoint, color is defined by differences in light wavelengths. So using color as a means of communication means recognizing and interpreting the light spectrum to convey meaning. The range of visible light humans can perceive spans roughly from 330 to 750 nanometers, which corresponds to the familiar order of rainbow colors.

This exhibition began with a sentence from Kim’s story. It imagines how a Korean astronaut and an alien species might try to communicate if they ever encountered one another. As part of this thought experiment, I assigned specific parts of the Korean writing system—initial, medial, and final consonants—to individual values on the light spectrum. I then organized this system into a large Excel spreadsheet, essentially creating a basic artificial language structure. Each character component was given a unique nanometer value and an RGB code, which I later used to determine the colors in the graphic work.

What took the most time wasn’t the visuals themselves, but building the system and organizing the numbers behind it. In most graphic design, colors are chosen based on instinct or aesthetic preference. But in this project, every color came directly from pre-defined spreadsheet values. It was a way to eliminate guesswork and explore what a visual language might look like if it were based entirely on structure and logic.

There were 20 artists in the show, and each of us was given our own little space and time to share what matters most to us right now—kind of like how the OS allocates time to each user. That diagram made me think, This needs to be turned into type. It felt perfect as a starting point for the visual language. I focused on things like line weights, dotted lines, and dash lengths—as if they were adjustable settings. I manually tweaked the stroke, dash, and offset values in Illustrator to get everything just right. Honestly, the whole process was super structured and kind of mechanical—maybe the most artificial thing I’ve ever made.

SM: How was the overall vibe of the exhibition? Anything that stood out?
CA: It was really fun! There were 18 artists in total, and even though it was in one building, each floor felt like stepping into a totally different world.
One moment I remember clearly—a visitor told me they were really moved by the show. They said each piece was bold and full of personality, and it gave them energy and courage in their own life. That really stuck with me. It made me think, Yeah, this was worth doing.

SM: The word artificial can sound kind of negative. What’s your take on that?
CA: I don’t think artificial is a bad thing at all. And I don’t believe “natural” is always better either. Honestly, all the connections in this show were technically artificial—we met because of the exhibition. The artists, the audience, performers, staff, friends—we all came together under one shared reason, and when it was over, we went back to our own lives. These days, so-called “natural” interactions are getting rare. We spend more time looking at our phones than at each other, and we keep up with our friends through Instagram. So maybe artificial interactions are just the new normal. And I think that’s okay. As long as there’s real sincerity in the moment, even an artificial friend is still a true friend.

SM: What direction do you want to head in moving forward?
CA: Honestly, I just want to keep making work—finding excuses to create. I hope I’ll be working on something again next year. If not, I might end up growing lettuce in Yangpyeong. I don’t have a big plan mapped out. I think more about what kind of attitude I want to have and what motivates me to keep doing this. Being a designer means constantly balancing practical concerns with ethical questions, so I’ve been reading books lately. And it doesn’t have to be limited to print or graphics. Who knows—it might turn into an installation next time.
SM: 자기소개 부탁드린다.
CA: 안채연이다. 그래픽 디자인을 하고 있으며, 이번 인공친구OS 전시의 아이덴티티 및 포스터 디자인을 맡았다.

SM: 이번 전시는 어떤 컨셉으로 작업했는가?
CA: 이번 전시는 Time Sharing Operating System(시분할 운영체제)에서 출발한 기획이다. 나 역시 처음에는 이 용어가 생소해서 구글 이미지 검색을 해보았는데, 중앙에는 원형 CPU가 있고 원 둘레에는 여러 유저들이 배치되어 있는 다이어그램이 나왔다. 여러 사용자가 하나의 컴퓨터를 공유하며 시간 단위로 자원을 할당받는 구조를 시각화한 이미지였다. 이 다이어그램을 보자마자 이번 전시의 의도를 직관적으로 이해할 수 있었다. 전시에 참여한 20명의 작가가 각자에게 주어진 시공간 안에서 지금 이 순간 가장 소중한 것을 시각화한다는 구조와 잘 맞아떨어졌다. 이 다이어그램을 보고 곧바로 ‘타입으로 구현해야겠다’는 생각이 들었다. 시각 언어의 키 비주얼로 사용하기에 적절하다고 판단했다. 선의 굵기 차이, 점선 처리, 대시의 간격 같은 요소들은 모두 조절 가능한 세팅 값으로 생각하며 작업을 진행했다. 일러스트레이터에서 Stroke, Dash, Offset 값을 하나하나 손으로 조정하고 출력하는 방식이었다. 작업 과정 전체가 컴퓨터적이고 기계적인 질서 안에서 이루어졌기 때문에, 어쩌면 지금까지 만든 작업 중 가장 ‘인공스러운’ 결과물일지도 모르겠다.

SM: 이번 전시의 분위기나 느낀 점은 어땠나?
CA: 18명의 작가와 함께한 전시였고, 한 건물 안에서도 층마다 완전히 다른 공간에 들어온 듯한 경험을 할 수 있어서 재밌었다. 특히 기억에 남는 순간이 있다. 어떤 관람객이 전시를 보고 감동을 받았다고 이야기했다. 각 작업마다 작가의 개성이 뚜렷하고 진취적인 시도들이 돋보였으며, 그 안에서 삶에 대한 용기와 자극을 받았다고 말했다. 그 이야기를 듣고 이번 전시를 하길 잘했다는 생각이 들었다.

SM: ‘인공적인’이라는 단어가 부정적으로 들릴 수도 있는데, 어떻게 생각하나?
CA: 나는 인공적인 것이 꼭 부정적인 의미를 가지는 것은 아니라고 생각한다. 자연적인 것이 더 우월하다는 생각도 하지 않는다. 이번 전시를 통해 만난 관계들도 모두 ‘전시’라는 명분을 통해 만들어진 인공적인 만남이었다. 작가, 관람객, 공연자, 스태프, 주변 지인들까지 모두 전시를 매개로 모였고, 전시가 끝난 뒤엔 각자의 자리로 돌아갔다. 요즘은 자연스러운 만남 자체가 점점 줄어들고 있다고 느낀다. 우리는 서로의 얼굴보다 핸드폰 화면을 더 오래 보고 있고, 안부는 인스타그램으로 확인하고 있다. 앞으로는 이러한 인공적인 방식이 새로운 소통의 형태가 될 수 있다고 생각한다. 어떤 방식이든, 어떤 계기든, 그 순간에 진심이 담겨 있다면 인공친구도 진짜 친구다.

SM: 앞으로의 방향성은?
CA: 무엇보다도 계속 작업을 이어갈 수 있는 계기를 만드는 것이 중요하다고 생각한다. 내년에도 작업할 수 있었으면 좋겠다. 그렇지 않으면 양평에서 상추를 기르고 있을지도 모르겠다. 추구하는 방향을 거창하게 설정하진 않는다. 오히려 내가 어떤 태도를 가져야 하는지, 어떤 명분으로 작업을 이어가야 하는지에 대해 자주 고민한다. 현실적인 고민과 윤리적인 가치를 함께 안고 가야 한다고 생각해서 요즘은 책도 많이 읽고 있다. 그 안에 내가 중요하게 생각하는 가치와 시각적 자극이 담겨 있을 것이다. 형식은 인쇄물이나 그래픽에만 국한되지 않을 것이다. 경우에 따라서는 설치작업이 될 수도 있다고 생각한다.
